## Core Research Findings

### 1. The 69% Rule -- Most Conflicts Are Perpetual

"69% of all relationship conflict problems are perpetual, which means they never go away. They never get fully solved. Conflict really mostly needs to be managed rather than solved."

"The couples who came back year after year kept bringing up exactly the same issue even 20 years later."

**Implication:** Stop trying to "fix" recurring conflicts. Learn to talk about them better. The goal is a temporary compromise, not a permanent solution.

### 2. The Four Horsemen of the Apocalypse

Four negative patterns that predict divorce (avg 6.2 years after wedding):

1. **Criticism** -- stating the problem as a defect in the partner's personality ("Our problem is that you're thoughtless and unkind")
2. **Defensiveness** -- counterattacking ("You're not so perfect. Here's what's wrong with you")
3. **Contempt** -- escalation with superiority, sarcasm, insult
4. **Stonewalling** -- emotional withdrawal, tuning out

"Can a couple come back from those? Sure, when we know the alternatives."

### 3. Masters vs Disasters

"What is the biggest mistake that the disasters of relationships make? They fight to win, which means somebody has to lose. What do the masters do instead? They fight to understand."

### 4. 90% of Problems Come from Before You Met

"How often are the problems rooted in the past versus the present relationship? 90%."

### 5. The Six Questions for Gridlock

When stuck on an issue, ask each other:
1. Do you have any ethics, beliefs, or values that are part of your position?
2. Is there any childhood or background history that is part of your position?
3. What are ALL your feelings about this issue?
4. Why is this so important to you?
5. What is your ideal dream here?
6. Is there some underlying life purpose or sense of meaning related to your position?

"These six questions are really powerful -- 87% of the time they move people out of gridlock."

### 6. Compatibility Is a Myth

"So many people believe in the myth that you have to be compatible in order to have a good relationship. It's not about the content of those discussions. It's more about how do you talk about them?"

"If you married your clone, you would be bored out of your minds."

### 7. Boundaries Can Block Growth

"A lot of times when people talk about boundaries, it's a way of stopping discussion on an issue. The problem with that all-or-none approach doesn't allow for any flexibility. Relationships succeed by being interdependent, not separate and independent."

### 8. Every Marriage Is Cross-Cultural

"Every marriage is a cross-cultural experience. Even if people come from the same ethnic group, the same part of the country, they have different ways of creating meaning."

### 9. It Takes Practice Like an Instrument

"It's like learning to play an instrument. You have to practice. You have to rehearse. You have to make mistakes and recover from them."

### 10. John's Notebook Technique

"When Julie says the four dreaded words 'we need to talk,' I whip out my notebook and get my pen out and say 'I'm listening. Talk to me.' And I write down what she says. If I'm feeling defensive, I just keep writing. I slow it down so I can write every word. And as I'm writing, I kind of go, 'Huh, that's a good point. Interesting.' So I stay calm and less defensive."

### 11. Every Day Is Precious

"My dad died at 58. That taught me a huge lesson in how every single day is precious. Every night before I go to sleep, I think about isn't it incredible that I've got this person next to me? How cool is that?"
