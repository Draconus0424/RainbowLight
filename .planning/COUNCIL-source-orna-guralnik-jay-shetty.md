> Source: On Purpose with Jay Shetty podcast -- "Why Couples Really Fight"
> For: RainbowLight therapy council member research

## Core Therapeutic Philosophies

### 1. Otherness Is the Real Issue

"The real issue that couples face is that they're building a relationship or living with another person who's different from them. And that is really hard."

"How do you respond to otherness? How do you respond to someone who's different from you in the smallest way and in the biggest way?"

"There's a space of difference and you don't understand why you're different and you immediately start building theories that are basically meant to answer the question: how am I right and what's wrong with you?"

### 2. Communication Problems Are Symptoms, Not Causes

"Couples typically think, if only we could communicate better, our problems would be solved. Usually there are real issues underlying the issues of communication. Often people make communication difficult because it's hard for them to touch the real issues."

### 3. Pull Back from the Partner, Look at Yourself

"Start getting curious about yourself rather than hyperfocus on your partner. Why does this thing bother me so much? What is the thing that is making me crazy? Usually you find really interesting things there."

### 4. Conflicting Loyalties Are the Hidden Driver

"It brings up conflicting loyalties -- loyalty to your family of origin, loyalty to your culture, loyalty to yourself, your sense of goodness, versus your loyalty to taking care of your partner. That's a real inner conflict."

"To be in a state of inner conflict is difficult. We like simple solutions. We like right or wrong. We like good or bad."

### 5. Create Safety Before Seeking Solutions

"What matters to you in a deep way is heard, is respected. You may not necessarily get exactly your way, but you feel on a basic human level that your dignity, the things you care about, are seen and respected."

"When people feel cared for in this way, they're willing to do a lot. We have an incredibly generous creative spirit that if we stop feeling threatened, we want to operate from there."

### 6. Equal Partners, Not Right vs Wrong

"Couples need to abandon the idea that one of them is completely right and one of them is wrong. You want to approach the difference with the idea that you're equal partners."

### 7. Don't Use Labels as Endpoints

"Gaslighting, triggers, trauma, love bombing -- the way people use these words is they have a feeling about something but they don't exactly know what that something is. So they grab a word that supposedly captures it and then they're done thinking."

"The moment you're thinking I found a word that finds all the problem outside of me, you're deluding yourself."

### 8. Relationships Are Growth and Service, Not Pleasure and Ease

Jay Shetty: "Relationships have to be about growth and service. And I think we think that they're about pleasure and ease."

"Growth is the opposite of ease and comfort. Service is the opposite of self-centeredness."

### 9. The Atmosphere Test

"When you spend time with a couple, there's an atmosphere around them that is palpable. Is it a music of mutual respect, adoration? Or is it a music of gotcha, this is where you failed?"

### 10. Stinginess Is Destructive

"Anything that makes people more stingy, I think is problematic. Stinginess, withholding is destructive. Give more. Give, give, give, give freely. Create a good environment around you."

### 11. Mine vs Ours

"The deep question that couples deal with is mine versus ours. What's mine and what are we sharing? Money is the most concrete way, but it's everything -- time, attention, air time, sex."

### 12. The Desire Beneath the Desire

"A lot of it is about wanting to feel desire of some sort. The experience of desire and being desired. That is really the thing we all want. To be living in desire rather than living in a certain kind of deadness."

### 13. Stay in Your Own Lane

"Don't intrude on someone else's journey. Stay in your own lane. Do the thing you're supposed to do and live and let live. That's the best way to create a good environment for a relationship."

### 14. Couples That Don't Argue Scare Me

"What? How do you not never argue? Have you become enmeshed with each other and everything about you that is different you just repress or dissociate? Are you so afraid of conflict? Have you vanished?"

### 15. Compatibility Is Overrated

"I used to believe that compatibility is the most important thing. And now I think the capacity to love someone who's different from you is the best source of growth."

## Jay Shetty's Relationship Principles (From the Interview)

### Healthy Relationship Markers
1. Both people don't expect the other to value what they value, but they allow each other's values and respect them
2. How we deal with new information and change -- a strong relationship allows for different iterations of each other
3. Don't depend on your partner for everything -- have other people too, but when it's about the relationship, go to your partner
4. Some days you carry them, some days they carry you -- don't count every day for 50/50

### Growth Over Comfort
- "I've become a better human because of this relationship. More self-aware. Less egotistical. More conscious of my flaws. In a less judgmental way."
- "The person you said you wanted to love forever becomes the person who actually gets the least loving part of you."

### The Home Design Metaphor
- "You grew up in a certain home and your partner grew up in a certain home. When you move in together, you design it to your tastes together. You're not trying to mirror those homes perfectly."

## Candidate Council Members (from this source)

### Psychoanalytic Lens (modeled on Orna Guralnik)
- Looks beneath surface complaints to find real issues
- Tracks conflicting loyalties
- Watches for repetition of childhood patterns
- Asks: "What is this REALLY about?"
- Red flag: using labels as endpoints instead of starting points

### Gottman Method Lens (referenced but not deeply covered)
- Four Horsemen: criticism, contempt, stonewalling, defensiveness
- Repair attempts -- who initiates, how quickly, does it land?
- Love Maps -- knowledge of partner's inner world
- Turning towards vs turning away

### Attachment Lens (referenced)
- Pursue-withdraw cycles
- Abandonment fears
- The "mine vs ours" tension as attachment expression
- Creating safety as prerequisite to vulnerability

### Growth & Values Lens (from Jay Shetty)
- Growth and service vs pleasure and ease
- Carrying each other in seasons
- Allowing different iterations of each other
- The home design metaphor -- building something new together
